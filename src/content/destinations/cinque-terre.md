# Cinque Terre: Five Villages Painted Into the Cliffs

Cinque Terre is proof that some of the world's most extraordinary places are also some of its smallest. Five fishing villages -- Riomaggiore, Manarola, Corniglia, Vernazza, and Monterosso al Mare -- cling to a rugged stretch of the Ligurian coast, connected by ancient footpaths that wind through terraced vineyards, past olive groves, and along cliff edges that plunge straight into the Mediterranean.

We remember our first glimpse of Manarola from the coastal trail -- a cascade of sherbet-colored houses tumbling down a rocky promontory into a harbor the size of a swimming pool, fishing boats pulled up on the rocks, laundry strung between windows. It looked like a painting that had somehow become three-dimensional. That feeling of unreality never quite goes away in Cinque Terre, no matter how many times we return.

These villages were largely inaccessible by road until the 1960s, and that isolation preserved something precious: a way of life built around fishing, winemaking, and terraced agriculture that stretches back over a thousand years. Today, Cinque Terre is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a national park, and while the villages have embraced tourism, they have not surrendered their character to it. The fishermen still go out at dawn, the vineyards still produce their distinctive Sciacchetra wine, and the trattorias still serve pesto made the way it has been made here for generations.

## The Five Villages

### Riomaggiore

The southernmost village is often the first one travelers encounter, arriving by train from La Spezia. Riomaggiore stacks itself vertically up a steep ravine, its tower houses painted in every shade of yellow, pink, terracotta, and ochre. The main street (Via Colombo) runs along the bottom of the ravine, lined with small shops, gelaterias, and restaurants, before emerging at a tiny harbor carved into the rocks.

Riomaggiore is the most "lived-in" feeling of the five villages. There is a working-class energy here that the others lack -- it feels less like a postcard and more like a real place where real people go about their days. The castle at the top of the village (free entry, but check opening hours) offers sweeping views along the coast, and the swimming rocks at the base of the harbor are the best free sunbathing spots in town.

Do not miss the Co-op grocery on Via Colombo, where you can assemble a picnic of local focaccia, cheese, olives, and wine for under €10 -- one of the best-value meals in all of Cinque Terre.

### Manarola

If we had to choose one village to represent Cinque Terre, it would be Manarola. This is the village that appears on every postcard, every travel magazine cover, every Instagram feed -- and it earns that attention honestly. The cluster of colorful houses perched on a dark rocky headland, with terraced vineyards rising steeply behind, is one of the most photographed scenes in Italy.

Manarola is the heart of Cinque Terre's winemaking tradition. The surrounding hillsides are covered in terraced vineyards producing Sciacchetra, a rare and prized dessert wine made from grapes dried on racks for months after harvest. Cantina Sociale di Riomaggiore-Manarola (the local cooperative winery) sells bottles from €8 for table wine to €25+ for Sciacchetra. Tastings can be arranged through the Cinque Terre Park office.

The Nessun Dorma terrace bar above the village has become famous for its views -- and rightfully so. A glass of local wine (€6-8) on their terrace at sunset, looking down over the village and out to sea, is one of those travel moments that stays with you. Arrive by 5:00 PM in summer to secure a spot.

### Corniglia

The middle village is the odd one out -- it sits on a hilltop 100 meters above the sea rather than at water level, and it is the only one without a harbor. Reaching it from the train station requires climbing 382 steps (the Lardarina stairway) or taking the shuttle bus (€2.50, or free with a Cinque Terre Card).

That effort filters out many day-trippers, making Corniglia the quietest and most authentic of the five villages. The main street (Via Fieschi) is a pleasure to walk, with local shops, a couple of excellent gelaterias, and views in both directions along the coast. Bar Terza Terra at the end of the promontory serves excellent coffee (€1.50) and has a terrace that feels like the prow of a ship sailing into the Ligurian Sea.

Corniglia is also home to the Guvano nudist beach, accessible through a disused railway tunnel -- though the tunnel has been officially closed for years, determined beachgoers still find their way down. The legitimate beach access is via a steep trail from the village.

### Vernazza

Vernazza is the jewel. Many seasoned Italy travelers consider it the most beautiful village in Cinque Terre, and after numerous visits, we are inclined to agree. It has the only natural harbor on this stretch of coast -- a crescent of calm water surrounded by tall, colorful buildings, with the medieval Doria Castle standing guard on the rocky promontory above.

The harbor piazza is the social center of the village, where locals and visitors mingle over aperitivi as fishing boats bob gently in the water. Vernazza was devastated by a flash flood in October 2011 that swept mud and debris through the village, but the community rebuilt with remarkable determination, and today the village is more beautiful than ever.

Climb up to the Doria Castle (€2) for the best aerial view of the village and harbor. The church of Santa Margherita d'Antiochia, with its distinctive octagonal bell tower, sits right on the waterfront and hosts the village's patron saint festival every July 20th with a spectacular fireworks display over the harbor.

For swimming, the small beach in the harbor is pleasant, or walk 10 minutes north on the trail toward Monterosso to find quieter rocky coves.

### Monterosso al Mare

The largest and most resort-like of the five villages, Monterosso has something the others lack: a proper sandy beach. The village is divided into the old town (centro storico) and the new town (Fegina) by a short tunnel, with the long beach stretching along the Fegina waterfront.

Monterosso is the best base for travelers who want beach time alongside their hiking and village-hopping. The beach is divided into free sections (spiaggia libera) and private stabilimenti where you can rent a sunbed and umbrella (€20-30/day for two loungers and a parasol). The water is clean and clear, and the swimming is excellent.

The old town has more character, with its narrow caruggi (alleys), the medieval church of San Giovanni Battista with its striking striped marble facade, and the ruins of the castle dividing old and new towns. The Giant statue (Il Gigante) on the Fegina beach -- a 14-meter statue of Neptune carved from concrete and steel in 1910 -- lost his arms and trident in a WWII bombing but still stands as a surreal landmark.

Monterosso also has the widest restaurant selection of any village and is where we recommend splurging on a proper seafood dinner.

## Hiking the Trails

Hiking is the defining experience of Cinque Terre. The trails connecting the villages have been used for centuries, and walking them is the way these communities were meant to be experienced.

### The Blue Trail (Sentiero Azzurro, Trail #2)

The famous Blue Trail connects all five villages along the coast. The full trail covers about 12 kilometers and takes 5-6 hours of walking. A Cinque Terre Trekking Card (€16/day or €29 for two days, includes unlimited train rides) is required for access. The trail is officially broken into four segments:

- **Riomaggiore to Manarola (Via dell'Amore):** This romantic 1-km paved path has been closed for landslide repairs for years. Check current status at the park office before visiting, as reopening has been anticipated.
- **Manarola to Corniglia:** 3 km, about 1.5 hours. Moderate difficulty with some steep sections and vineyard views.
- **Corniglia to Vernazza:** 3.5 km, about 2 hours. The most challenging and scenic section, with dramatic cliff-edge stretches.
- **Vernazza to Monterosso:** 3.5 km, about 2 hours. Strenuous in places with significant elevation gain, but stunning views throughout.

### Trail #6 (Sanctuary Path)

For more ambitious hikers, the high trail (Trail #6/6A) runs along the ridge above the villages, connecting a series of hilltop sanctuaries. It is longer and more strenuous than the Blue Trail but offers mountain and sea views simultaneously, passes through chestnut forests, and encounters far fewer people. No card is required for this trail. The full ridge walk is about 35 km and can be broken into day-hike segments.

## Where to Eat

### Trattoria dal Billy (Manarola) -- Best Seafood

Perched near the top of Manarola with views that stretch to the horizon, Dal Billy is our favorite restaurant in Cinque Terre. Run by the same family for generations, it serves superb seafood in generous portions. The mixed seafood antipasto (€18) is a masterclass in simplicity -- raw and marinated fish, octopus, anchovies, all impeccably fresh. Their handmade trofie al pesto (€14) uses basil grown in the family's own garden. Grilled catch of the day runs €18-24. A full meal with local Cinque Terre white wine comes to about €40-50 per person. Book the terrace table. Reservations essential.

### Ristorante Belforte (Vernazza) -- Romantic Splurge

Built into the medieval tower overlooking Vernazza's harbor, Belforte is one of the most atmospheric restaurants on the Ligurian coast. The setting alone -- stone walls, candlelight, crashing waves visible through the windows -- would justify a visit, but the food is genuinely excellent. Start with the Vernazza anchovies (€12), move to the lobster linguine (€28), and finish with the semifreddo (€8). Main courses run €18-30, and a full dinner with wine costs about €55-70 per person. Request the tower room for the most dramatic setting. Closed Tuesdays.

### Osteria a Cantina de Mananan (Corniglia) -- Authentic Local

This tiny osteria on Via Fieschi in Corniglia seats maybe twenty people in a vaulted stone cellar that feels unchanged from centuries past. The menu is short and changes daily based on what is available. Expect dishes like stuffed mussels (€10), rabbit with olives and pine nuts (€16), and the best farinata (chickpea flatbread, €5) we have found in Liguria. House wine is served in ceramic jugs (€4 per quarter-liter). A full meal costs about €25-30 per person. No reservations, no credit cards, no pretension -- just honest Ligurian cooking.

### Enoteca Dau Cila (Riomaggiore) -- Wine and Small Plates

Occupying a former fishing boat shelter right on Riomaggiore's harbor, Dau Cila is the perfect spot for a long lunch or early evening aperitivo. The wine list focuses on Cinque Terre DOC wines and Sciacchetra, with glasses from €5 and flights from €12. Pair your wine with taglieri (charcuterie boards, €12-16), bruschette with anchovy paste (€6), or a plate of fried calamari (€10). The setting is irresistible -- you are essentially sitting on the edge of the Mediterranean. Budget €20-30 per person for wine and shared plates.

### L'Ancora della Tortuga (Monterosso) -- Fine Dining

Monterosso's best restaurant occupies a terrace overlooking the beach in the old town. Chef prepares refined seafood dishes that elevate Ligurian tradition without abandoning it. The tasting menu (€65 per person, wine pairing €30 extra) is outstanding, featuring courses like scampi crudo with citrus, black squid ink risotto, and grilled branzino with Taggiasca olives. A la carte mains run €22-32. Service is polished but warm, and the sunset views from the terrace are sublime. Reservations essential. Budget €60-80 per person with wine.

## Where to Stay

### Budget: Ostello Cinque Terre (Manarola) -- From €30/night dorm

The only proper hostel in the five villages sits above Manarola with panoramic views from its terrace. Dorm rooms (4-6 beds) are basic but clean, and there is a communal kitchen and laundry facilities. The terrace is a major social hub in the evenings. Reception closes at midnight. The hostel also offers simple private rooms from €70/night. Book months ahead for summer dates -- this place fills fast.

### Budget: Locanda dalla Compagnia (Vernazza) -- From €75/night

A small guesthouse on Vernazza's main piazza run by a welcoming local family. Rooms are simple but comfortable, with good beds and functioning air conditioning. The location is unbeatable -- you can hear the harbor waves from your window. No breakfast, but you are steps from several cafes. The family also rents apartments in the village from €90/night, ideal for longer stays.

### Mid-Range: La Mala (Vernazza) -- From €130/night

Four beautifully designed rooms clinging to the cliff above Vernazza's harbor, each with a private terrace offering staggering views. Room names reflect their position (Sea-View, Tower-View, etc.), and each is individually decorated with local artwork and quality linens. This is boutique accommodation at its finest, and the terrace breakfasts alone are worth the price. The owners are a wealth of local knowledge. Book several months ahead.

### Mid-Range: Hotel Marina (Monterosso) -- From €140/night

A well-run 3-star hotel in Monterosso's old town, steps from the beach and the train station. Rooms are modern and comfortable, with air conditioning and decent bathrooms. Sea-view rooms (€180/night) are worth the upgrade. The included breakfast buffet is generous by Italian standards. The hotel has partnerships with local boat operators and can arrange private coast tours.

### Luxury: Hotel Porto Roca (Monterosso) -- From €280/night

The only true luxury hotel in Cinque Terre occupies a dramatic cliff-side position above Monterosso, surrounded by Mediterranean gardens. Rooms are elegant with sea-view terraces, and the hotel restaurant serves excellent Ligurian cuisine with views. There is a bar terrace where sunset aperitivi feel ceremonial. The pool is a welcome luxury after a day of hiking. Half-board packages (€50 supplement) are excellent value.

## When to Visit

**April and May** are our preferred months. The trails are green and wildflower-covered, temperatures are comfortable (16-22°C), the sea is beginning to warm, and the villages have not yet reached summer saturation. May brings reliably sunny weather and is ideal for hiking.

**September and October** are equally excellent. Temperatures remain warm (18-25°C), the sea is at its warmest, the grape harvest brings festivals and activity to the vineyards, and the summer crowds thin dramatically after mid-September. October can bring some rain but also produces dramatic skies and spectacular light.

**June through August** is peak season. The villages are packed, the trails are crowded and hot (30°C+), trains are standing-room only, and accommodation prices double. The experience is still beautiful but significantly more stressful. If you must visit in summer, stay in the villages rather than day-tripping from La Spezia, and do your hiking early in the morning.

**November through March** is off-season. Many hotels and restaurants close, weather is unpredictable with frequent rain, and some trails close. However, the villages take on a quiet, atmospheric beauty, and the locals have time to chat. A winter visit is best for travelers who prioritize authenticity over convenience.

## Getting Around

The **Cinque Terre Train** is the practical lifeline connecting all five villages and La Spezia. Trains run every 15-30 minutes in season, and the journey between the outermost villages (Riomaggiore to Monterosso) takes about 20 minutes. The Cinque Terre Trekking Card (€16/day) includes unlimited train travel plus trail access -- it is the essential purchase for any visit.

**Ferries** run between the villages from April to October (not Corniglia, which has no harbor). A hop-on-hop-off day pass costs €35. The ferries are slower than the train but infinitely more scenic, and seeing the villages from the sea is a different experience entirely. Single trips cost €5-12 depending on distance.

**Walking** between villages on the Blue Trail takes 1-2 hours per segment, and this is the way we most strongly recommend experiencing Cinque Terre. The trails reveal coastal scenery, terraced landscapes, and dramatic perspectives that you simply cannot access any other way.

**Cars** are essentially useless in Cinque Terre. Roads to the villages are narrow and steep, parking is extremely limited and expensive (€15-25/day where available), and several villages restrict vehicle access. Leave your car in La Spezia (parking from €10/day near the station) and take the train.

## Scott's Pro Tips

**Buy the two-day Cinque Terre Trekking Card (€29).** It includes unlimited trains and trail access for 48 hours and saves significant money over buying individual tickets and trail entries. Pick it up at any Cinque Terre train station or park office.

**Do the Blue Trail from north to south.** Starting in Monterosso and walking to Riomaggiore means the sun is behind you in the morning (better for photos), and the most dramatic views face you as you walk. Most guided groups go south to north, so you will also be walking against the crowd flow.

**Stay in Vernazza or Manarola, not Monterosso.** Monterosso has the beach and the most hotels, but it also has the least charm. Vernazza and Manarola are where the magic of Cinque Terre is most concentrated, and both have excellent train connections.

**For the best pesto in Cinque Terre, look for mortar-and-pestle signs.** Genuine Genovese pesto is made by hand in a marble mortar with a wooden pestle -- the name "pesto" comes from "pestare" (to pound). Machine-made pesto is fine, but the hand-pounded version has a fresher, more complex flavor. Il Pirun in Vernazza makes their trofie al pesto this way (€12), and you can taste the difference.

**Hike the Corniglia-to-Vernazza segment if you only have time for one.** This 3.5-km stretch has the most dramatic scenery of the entire Blue Trail -- cliff edges, switchbacks through vineyards, and the stunning reveal of Vernazza appearing below you as you round the final headland. Allow two hours and carry water.

**Visit Corniglia for lunch.** Because it requires climbing 382 steps from the station (or taking the shuttle), most day-trippers skip it entirely. That means Corniglia at midday is peaceful, its restaurants are uncrowded, and prices are lower than elsewhere. It is the most authentic village experience in Cinque Terre.

**Watch the sunset from Nessun Dorma in Manarola, but skip dinner there.** The views are legendary, the drinks are fine, but the food is average and overpriced. Have a glass of wine (€6-8) for the sunset, then walk to Trattoria dal Billy for a proper dinner.

**Bring a refillable water bottle.** There are public drinking fountains in every village, and the water is clean and cold. Buying bottled water at €2-3 per bottle in shops adds up quickly, especially on hiking days.

## A Note on Overtourism

Cinque Terre has become one of Italy's most discussed examples of overtourism, with the villages collectively receiving over 2.5 million visitors per year despite having a combined resident population of under 5,000. The impact is real -- trail erosion, waste management challenges, housing pressure on locals, and a sometimes overwhelming crush of visitors in the narrow streets.

We can all be part of the solution. Visit in shoulder season when possible. Stay overnight in the villages rather than day-tripping (overnight visitors contribute far more to the local economy). Buy from local shops rather than generic souvenir stands. Stay on marked trails. Carry your rubbish out. And most importantly, slow down. Cinque Terre was not built for speed. It was built for fishing, farming, and living in harmony with an extraordinary landscape. The more we approach it on those terms, the better the experience for everyone -- visitors and residents alike.

## Final Thoughts

There is a moment on the trail between Corniglia and Vernazza when the path rounds a headland and Vernazza's harbor appears below -- tiny, perfect, impossibly colorful against the dark rock and deep blue sea. Every time we see it, we stop walking. Not to take a photograph, though we do that too, but simply to let the beauty of it register fully.

Cinque Terre is small. You can see all five villages in a single long day. But we would encourage you not to. Give it at least two or three days. Walk the trails slowly. Sit in the piazzas. Eat the pesto and drink the wine and watch the fishing boats come in. Let these villages reveal themselves at their own pace, and they will show you something that no amount of Instagram scrolling can replicate: the simple, profound beauty of a human community perfectly adapted to its landscape, still living and breathing after a thousand years.
