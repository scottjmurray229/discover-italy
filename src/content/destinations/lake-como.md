# Lake Como: Where the Alps Meet Italian Elegance

There is a moment on the ferry from Varenna to Bellagio when the full scale of Lake Como reveals itself. The Alps rise sharply on both sides, their peaks still streaked with snow in late spring. Grand villas with terracotta roofs line the shore, their gardens tumbling down to the waterline in cascades of wisteria and oleander. The lake itself — 146 meters deep and impossibly blue — stretches north toward Switzerland, disappearing into a haze of mountain and sky. It is, without exaggeration, one of the most beautiful landscapes in Europe.

We have visited Lake Como across several seasons and always find ourselves recalibrating our expectations upward. Photographs cannot capture the luminous quality of the light here, the way the mountains change color through the day, or the particular pleasure of arriving at a waterfront village by boat rather than by road. This is a place that operates on a different register — slower, more elegant, and more deeply satisfying than the word "lake holiday" might suggest.

## Why Visit Lake Como

### A Landscape That Defies Superlatives

Lake Como's Y-shaped form creates an ever-changing panorama. The narrow northern arm pushes deep into the Alps, the southwestern branch reaches toward the flatlands of Lombardy, and the southeastern branch stretches toward Lecco. At the intersection of these three arms sits the Bellagio promontory, often called the most beautiful point on the most beautiful lake in the world. We would not argue with that assessment.

The vertical drama is what sets Como apart from other Italian lakes. Mountains rise 1,500 meters or more directly from the waterline, creating a fjord-like intensity that feels almost Scandinavian — until you notice the lemon trees, the palm trees, and the decidedly Mediterranean pace of life.

### Centuries of Villa Culture

Since Roman times, the wealthy and powerful have built retreats on the shores of Lake Como. Pliny the Younger had two villas here in the first century. The tradition continued through the Renaissance, the Baroque era, and into the present day — George Clooney's Villa Oleandra in Laglio is merely the latest chapter in a very long story.

Many of the grandest villas are open to visitors, and their gardens alone justify the trip. Villa Carlotta, Villa Melzi, Villa del Balbianello, and Villa Monastero each offer a different expression of the Italian garden tradition, set against a backdrop that no garden designer could improve upon.

### The Ferry Network

One of Lake Como's greatest pleasures is its public ferry system, operated by Navigazione Laghi. The network of ferries, hydrofoils, and car ferries connects dozens of lakeside towns, and using them is not just practical — it is the single most enjoyable way to experience the lake. Sitting on the open deck of a slow ferry, watching the shoreline unfold village by village, villa by villa, is a form of travel that belongs to an earlier, more gracious age.

## Top Things to Do at Lake Como

### Visit Villa del Balbianello

Perched on a wooded promontory at the tip of the Lavedo peninsula near Lenno, Villa del Balbianello is Lake Como's most photogenic building — and the competition is fierce. The villa's layered terraces, loggia with triple arches, and impossibly romantic gardens have appeared in films from Star Wars to Casino Royale. The interior houses a collection of exploration memorabilia assembled by its last owner, the adventurer Guido Monzino, who led the first Italian expedition to Everest.

**Practical info:** Entry to the gardens costs €10; gardens plus villa interior €20. Open Tuesday to Sunday, mid-March through mid-November. You can reach the villa by a 1-kilometer walk from Lenno (partly on an uneven path) or by small boat from Lenno or Sala Comacina (approximately €7 round trip). Go early or late to avoid the midday crowds.

### Explore Bellagio

Bellagio earns its nickname "the Pearl of the Lake" with cobblestone lanes that climb steeply from the waterfront, elegant boutiques and restaurants tucked into stone archways, and views in every direction that seem designed for oil paintings. It is undeniably touristy, but it has earned that tourism through genuine beauty rather than manufactured charm.

Stroll the waterfront promenade, climb the stepped alleyways of the Salita Serbelloni, visit the gardens of Villa Melzi (€8 entry, open late March through October), and end with an aperitivo at one of the lakefront bars watching the ferry traffic. Budget half a day minimum; a full day is better.

### Ferry Hopping Around the Central Lake

Buy a one-day ferry pass (€15 for the central lake zone) and spend a day hopping between villages. A perfect circuit: start in Varenna, ferry to Bellagio (15 min), ferry to Cadenabbia or Tremezzo (15 min) to visit Villa Carlotta (€12 entry, spectacular gardens), ferry to Lenno for Villa del Balbianello, then return to Varenna for dinner. The slow ferries are cheaper and more scenic; the hydrofoils (aliscafi) are faster and slightly more expensive.

### Walk the Greenway del Lago

This 10-kilometer walking path follows the western shore of the lake from Colonno to Cadenabbia, passing through olive groves, tiny fishing villages, Romanesque churches, and villa gardens. The terrain is gentle and mostly flat, and the views are consistently spectacular. Budget 3-4 hours for the full walk, or do a shorter section between any two villages and catch a ferry back. Free, no tickets required — just comfortable shoes.

### Wander Varenna's Waterfront

Varenna is Lake Como's most romantic small town — less polished than Bellagio, less busy than Como, and more authentic than either. The Passeggiata degli Innamorati (Lovers' Walk) is a narrow boardwalk bolted to the cliffs above the lake, connecting the ferry dock to the main piazza. The 13th-century church of San Giorgio overlooks a tiny harbor where fishing boats bob on water so clear you can count the stones on the bottom.

Visit the gardens of Villa Monastero (€8 entry) — a 2-kilometer botanical garden running along the lakeshore — and climb to the ruined Castello di Vezio (€4 entry) above the town for panoramic views. Varenna is also home to some of the lake's best restaurants and most reasonably priced accommodation.

### Take the Funicular to Brunate

From the town of Como, a creaking funicular railway (€6 round trip) climbs steeply to the village of Brunate, perched 715 meters above the lake. The seven-minute ride is dramatic enough, but the views from the top are extraordinary — on a clear day, the panorama extends from Monte Rosa in the west to the Bernina massif in the east. From Brunate, hiking trails lead higher into the mountains, including the path to the Faro Voltiano lighthouse (30 min each way).

## Where to Eat at Lake Como

### Crotto dei Platani — Varenna (€€)

Via Peschiera, 5. Set directly on the lake with tables practically in the water, Crotto dei Platani serves refined Lombard cuisine with an emphasis on freshwater fish. The risotto with perch fillets (€16) is delicate and perfectly executed. The lake-view terrace is one of the most romantic dining spots on Como. A full dinner with wine runs €40-55 per person. Reservations essential in season.

### Trattoria San Giacomo — Bellagio (€€)

Salita Serbelloni, 45. Tucked into one of Bellagio's steep lanes, this family-run trattoria offers honest Lombard cooking at fair prices in a town where many restaurants charge a premium for the location. Homemade casoncelli pasta (€12), lake fish grilled simply with herbs (€16), and a tiramisù (€7) that has earned a loyal following. A full meal costs €30-40 per person. Closed Wednesdays.

### Locanda dell'Isola Comacina — Ossuccio (€€€)

Isola Comacina. This unique restaurant occupies Lake Como's only island and has served the same fixed menu since 1947. You arrive by private boat from Ossuccio (included in the price), dine on a sequence of dishes ending with an elaborate fire ceremony involving coffee, sugar, and grappa, and depart feeling that you have experienced something genuinely singular. The fixed menu costs approximately €75-85 per person including the boat. Open March through October. Reserve well in advance — this is a bucket-list dining experience.

### Bar Il Molo — Varenna (€)

Piazza San Giorgio. The best spot in Varenna for a morning coffee, a lunchtime panino (€5-8), or an evening Aperol Spritz (€7). The terrace sits right on the main piazza above the water, and the friendly owners make everyone feel welcome. A casual meal here costs €10-18 per person. No reservations needed — arrive early for the best lakefront tables.

### Ristorante Bilacus — Bellagio (€€)

Salita Serbelloni, 32. A well-priced option in Bellagio's center, Bilacus serves regional dishes in a vaulted stone dining room. The missoltini (dried lake fish, a Como specialty, €10) and polenta with braised beef (€14) are worth trying. A full dinner costs €25-35 per person. Open for lunch and dinner; closed Tuesdays.

## Where to Stay at Lake Como

### Budget: Ostello Bello Lake Como — From €35/Night (Dorm) / €80 (Private)

Viale Fratelli Rosselli, 9, Como. Part of the excellent Ostello Bello chain, this hostel near Como's train station offers cheerful dorms and private rooms, a rooftop terrace, and a sociable atmosphere. Free pasta dinner several nights a week. The location at the southern end of the lake is ideal for reaching the ferry network. Dorms from €35, private doubles from €80.

### Mid-Range: Hotel du Lac — Varenna — From €140/Night

Via del Prestino, 11. A family-run hotel with the best lakefront position in Varenna — rooms look directly over the water, and the restaurant terrace is one of the most photographed spots on the lake. Rooms are comfortable and clean without being luxurious, but you are paying for the location, and it is worth every cent. Doubles from €140-220 depending on view and season. Book well ahead for lake-view rooms.

### Luxury: Grand Hotel Tremezzo — From €400/Night

Via Regina, 8, Tremezzo. If you are going to splurge at Lake Como, the Grand Hotel Tremezzo is the place to do it. This Art Nouveau palace sits directly across the lake from Bellagio, with a floating swimming pool on the lake, a full-service spa, and grounds that would make a Medici envious. The T Spa is extraordinary. Doubles from €400; suites from €800. Open April through November.

## Practical Tips for Lake Como

<div class="scott-tips">

### Scott's Tips for Lake Como

**Base yourself in Varenna, not Bellagio.** Varenna is more affordable, less crowded, equally beautiful, and better connected by train to Milan. The ferry to Bellagio takes just 15 minutes, so you can easily visit without paying Bellagio prices for accommodation.

**Download the Navigazione Laghi ferry timetable.** The paper timetable posted at docks can be confusing. The app and website (navigazionelaghi.it) let you plan connections with precision. Ferries run less frequently in the shoulder season, so check schedules carefully.

**Avoid driving around the lake if possible.** The lakeside roads are narrow, winding, and frequently choked with traffic in summer. Parking in lakeside towns ranges from difficult to impossible. The train-plus-ferry combination is faster, cheaper, and infinitely more enjoyable.

**Carry a rain layer even in summer.** Lake Como creates its own microclimate, and afternoon showers can materialize quickly, especially when the breva wind shifts. Mornings are almost always calmer than afternoons on the water.

**Book Villa del Balbianello early in the day.** By midday in peak season, the villa's terraces become uncomfortably crowded for photography. Arrive when it opens at 10:00 for the best experience.

**Rent a private boat if budget allows.** Self-drive motorboats can be rented at various points around the lake, typically €50-80 per hour without a license for low-powered boats. There is no better way to experience Como's shoreline at your own pace. Longer rentals with a captain run from €200-400 for a half day.

**Eat the local lake fish.** Missoltini (sun-dried shad), lavarello (whitefish), and pesce persico (perch) are Lake Como specialties that you will not find prepared this well anywhere else. Skip the pizza — you are in Lombardy, not Naples.

**Budget tip: picnic on the ferry.** Stock up on focaccia, cheese, and bresaola from a deli in Varenna or Bellagio, and eat your lunch on the deck of a slow ferry between villages. It is the best-value meal on the lake, with the best views.

**September is the golden month.** The summer crowds have thinned, the water is still warm enough for swimming (the lake retains heat well into autumn), and the light takes on that particular amber quality that makes every photograph look like a Renaissance painting. Hotel prices drop noticeably from their July-August peaks.

## Getting Around Lake Como

The Navigazione Laghi ferry system is the backbone of lake travel. Three types of vessels operate:

- **Battello (slow ferry):** Cheapest, most scenic, stops at every village. A single ticket from Varenna to Bellagio costs €4.60.
- **Aliscafo (hydrofoil):** Faster, roughly double the price, skips smaller stops. Good for longer distances.
- **Traghetto (car ferry):** Crosses the central lake between Bellagio, Varenna, Menaggio, and Cadenabbia. Foot passengers pay around €4.60 per crossing.

A one-day central lake pass (€15) offers excellent value if you plan to make three or more trips.

Buses operated by ASF Autolinee connect towns along the shoreline roads, and they are useful for reaching places the ferries skip, but the ferry is almost always the superior option.

## How Many Days Do You Need at Lake Como

We recommend a minimum of two full days at Lake Como: one for the central lake triangle (Varenna, Bellagio, Tremezzo) and one for the western shore (Villa del Balbianello, Greenway del Lago, possibly the Tremezzina). Three days allows for the town of Como itself, the Brunate funicular, and a more relaxed pace. Four to five days is ideal for combining sightseeing with genuine relaxation — swimming, reading on a lakefront terrace, lingering over long lunches with lake views.

Lake Como is not a place to rush through checking off sights. Its deepest pleasures are atmospheric: the play of light on water, the sound of ferry horns echoing off mountain walls, the slow drift of an afternoon spent doing very little at all. Give yourself time to feel it, not just see it.

## Final Thoughts

Lake Como represents a particular vision of Italian beauty — more restrained and refined than the exuberant south, more dramatic than the gentle Tuscan hills, and with a quality of light and landscape that has drawn artists, writers, and lovers for two thousand years. It is expensive compared to much of Italy, but the visual and emotional returns are enormous.

Come for the villas and the views. Stay for the ferry rides and the lake fish. Leave with a memory of blue water, green mountains, and terracotta rooftops that will glow in your imagination for years to come.
